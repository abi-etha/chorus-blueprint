# Frequently Asked Questions

---

## About the Framework

**What is Chorus?**
Chorus is a multi-agent deliberation framework designed to hold complex governance problems without flattening them. Each agent contributes a distinct analytical lens — ecological, political, human, structural — and the ensemble converges on conclusions that no single agent could reach alone. The name comes from music: a chorus is not a solo amplified, it is multiple distinct voices producing something together.

**What is the Chorus Blueprint?**
The governance framework that emerged from four rounds of Chorus simulation. It consists of five pillars: governance architecture, compute triage, ecological constraints, international coordination, and human primacy. It has been stress-tested against three crisis vectors and adversarially challenged by four contrarian positions.

**Who built this?**
The framework was developed through collaborative simulation across three AI systems — Claude (Anthropic), Grok (xAI), and Copilot (Microsoft) — and one human architect. The cross-model convergence on structural soundness was a meaningful signal. The core insight — human primacy as a governance principle — came from the human architect, not from any AI system.

**Is this finished?**
No. The ensemble grew across four rounds — Reed and Anthropos were not in the original simulation, and their absence shaped the early conclusions. The architecture is designed to be extended. Every governance framework that matters was incomplete at first publication.

## On Deliberate AI

**What does "deliberate AI" mean?**
Not less AI, not no AI — AI as a scalpel rather than a default replacement. AI where it genuinely cannot or should not be replaced by humans, and humans where it can. The current default deploys AI broadly and lets the market sort it. Deliberate AI inverts that default: priority is determined by human benefit, not economic return.

**Isn't "sustainability first" just anti-progress?**
No. The Tempo KPI was added specifically to address this concern. If deliberate AI governance causes innovation velocity to decline in Tier 1–3 domains — medical AI, climate modeling, scientific discovery — the framework self-corrects through automatic review and possible temporary easing of constraints in those domains. Sustainability first does not mean innovation last. It means innovation with full-cost accounting.

**What's wrong with AI entertainment and content generation?**
Nothing is banned. Non-priority applications including entertainment and high-volume content generation are placed in a lower triage tier, meaning they are rationed first under any compute constraint condition and require HSI justification for large-scale deployment. The argument is not that entertainment has no value — it is that under scarcity, climate modeling and drug discovery have more claim on compute resources than AI-generated content at scale.

## On Governance

**Who would actually run this?**
The blueprint proposes a Federated Governance Network with three tiers: Regional Deliberation Bodies, a KPI Standards Council, and an Independent Verification Office. Phase 0 is voluntary — willing organizations and jurisdictions adopt the framework and build proof points. Phase 1 formalizes the structure. The goal is to make compliance the path of least resistance, not to mandate compliance from a standing start.

**How do you prevent the governance body from being captured?**
That is Reed's question, and it is the central design challenge of the blueprint. The structural mechanisms are: rotating seats with mandatory gaps, full funding disclosure, mandatory recusal, an ethics panel selected by lottery, a proxy and lobbyist ban, sunset clauses on all rules, a compute levy that funds governance independently of industry, and public contestability of every decision. No mechanism eliminates capture risk entirely. Together they make capture expensive, visible, and reversible.

**What about countries that don't participate?**
Bastion raised this directly in the adversarial round. The treaty network addresses it through structured trade friction for non-signatories on chip and energy infrastructure imports, and safe harbor provisions that reward compliance with interoperability. "The adversary won't comply" has been used to block every arms control agreement before it was signed. Meaningful arms control agreements have been signed. The framework is designed for the structure of relationships, not for any specific political moment.

**Why does the Global South get veto power?**
Because governance that does not include the people most affected by its decisions is not governance — it is the imposition of constraints by those with power on those without. Meridian's challenge was the hardest in the adversarial round and it stands. The Global South bears the greatest ecological cost of AI infrastructure while having the least control over it. A governance framework designed without Global South authorship will serve the interests of whoever designed it.

## On Human Primacy

**Aren't humans inefficient compared to AI in many domains?**
Yes, in narrow efficiency terms, often. That is not a sufficient reason to remove humans from those domains. Human labor carries value beyond economic output — skill development, dignity, community resilience, meaning. The Meaningful Work Compact formalizes this recognition. Full-cost accounting that includes human displacement impact produces different optimization targets than accounting that treats efficiency as the only variable.

**Does human primacy mean AI can't be used in healthcare?**
No. AI assistance in healthcare is explicitly protected as a Tier 2 priority. The Right to Human Process means that humans retain the right to human-mediated processes in consequential decisions — a radiologist reviewing an AI-flagged scan, a doctor making a treatment decision with AI-generated analysis. AI assists. AI does not replace without consent.

**What if humans are actually worse at something than AI?**
Then the HSI score will reflect that — a low HSI means AI is clearly necessary and the standard approval process applies. The Anthropos Gate is not a presumption that humans are always better. It is a requirement that the question be asked and answered with evidence before deployment proceeds.

## On the Simulation Methodology

**Why use multiple AI systems?**
Claude, Grok, and Copilot have genuinely different training approaches, alignment philosophies, and adversarial profiles. When structurally distinct systems with different incentives converge on the same core conclusions, that convergence is meaningful. It is not proof — it is a significant prior. Cross-model validation was chosen precisely because no single AI system should be trusted as the sole validator of a governance framework for AI systems.

**Can I run Chorus myself?**
Yes. The [framework documentation](../framework/chorus-architecture.md) describes how the ensemble works. The [reflex definitions](../framework/reflex-definitions.md) define each agent's role, constraint, and lens. You can run Chorus on any complex governance or systems problem by instantiating the ensemble in any AI system capable of multi-role reasoning.

**Can I extend Chorus with new agents?**
Yes — and you should. Reed and Anthropos were not in the original ensemble. Their addition made the framework meaningfully better. If you identify a gap that no existing agent covers, that gap deserves a new agent. Document the role, constraint, and lens, and open a pull request.
