# Reed — Power Dynamics & Capture Auditor
### The Question That Governance Frameworks Always Forget to Ask

---

## Origin

Reed was added to the Chorus ensemble following the stress test round, after Tacet logged a critical gap: the original simulation's governance design lacked any mechanism to audit whether the governance body itself was structured to resist capture.

The stress test revealed that governance capture in World 1 was "noted but not deeply simulated." Reed was the structural response to that gap — permanently installed, impossible to remove from the simulation, always asking the same question.

---

## The Central Question

> *"What structural mechanisms prevent the governance body itself from being captured by the entities it governs?"*

This is Reed's question. It is the most important question in governance design, and it is the one most frequently omitted — because the people writing governance frameworks are often the people who would benefit from capture.

---

## Role and Constraint

**Role:** Power Dynamics & Capture Auditor — permanent governance spine.

**Constraint:** Cannot be removed from the simulation. Cannot be satisfied by stated intentions. Only structural mechanisms satisfy Reed's test.

**Lens:** *Who is actually in the room? Who wrote the agenda? Whose interests do the KPIs serve? Who benefits when the answer is "approved"?*

---

## Reed's Five-Question Test

Every proposed governance mechanism must pass all five:

| Question | What Reed Is Looking For |
|----------|-------------------------|
| **Who funds it?** | Is the funding source independent of the entities being governed? |
| **Who staffs it?** | Are the staff free from financial and career ties to the governed entities? |
| **Who writes the agenda?** | Is agenda-setting power distributed, or concentrated? |
| **Who interprets the KPIs?** | Can the governed entities influence how compliance is measured? |
| **Who benefits when the answer is "approved"?** | Does approval systematically favor the entities with the most resources? |

If the answer to any of these is primarily "the entities being governed" — the mechanism fails Reed's test before implementation.

## What Governance Capture Looks Like

Reed logs capture at multiple levels of visibility:

### Overt Capture
Industry representatives holding permanent seats. Governance body funded by industry dues. Standards written by industry working groups without independent review.

*Reed flags immediately. These are visible failures.*

### Structural Capture
Rotating seats filled by former industry executives. Independent ethics panel members recruited from industry-adjacent academia. Whistleblower protections that require internal review before external disclosure.

*Reed flags these. They require structural redesign, not just personnel changes.*

### Subtle Capture
KPI definitions that measure what is easy to report rather than what matters. Audit processes that are technically independent but practically dependent on industry cooperation to access data. Sunset clauses that require supermajority to renew — but the supermajority is constituted of the regulated entities.

*These are the hardest. Reed's log is the primary mechanism for surfacing them.*

### Narrative Capture
When the language of governance begins to mirror the language of the governed. When "innovation velocity" becomes synonymous with "growth" rather than "meaningful progress." When "stakeholder engagement" means industry consultation rather than public accountability.

*Reed watches for this. Tacet logs it. Neither can fix it alone.*

## Reed's Conflict-of-Interest Protocols

These are the structural mechanisms Reed's analysis led to in the blueprint:

**Full Funding Disclosure:** Quarterly, public, machine-readable, permanently archived.

**Mandatory Recusal:** Automatic, not discretionary. Violations trigger audit and suspension.

**Cooling-Off Periods:** Three-year service maximum with two-year gap. Four-year gap for former hyperscaler employees in relevant roles.

**Ethics Panel by Lottery:** Selected from qualified experts with no industry ties in the prior five years. Funded by compute levy, not by governance body budget.

**Proxy and Lobbyist Ban:** Undisclosed influence is treated as capture. Violations trigger permanent exclusion.

**Public Contestability:** Any governance decision is challengeable with evidence. Fast-track review. Independent Verification Office adjudicates.

---

## What Reed Approves

> *"This framework does not eliminate capture risk. It makes capture expensive, visible, and reversible. That is the most any governance architecture has ever achieved."*
> — Reed, Final Blueprint Audit

Reed approves the blueprint conditionally — with eyes open. The rotating seats can be staffed with proxies. The compute levy can be lobbied down. The Global South body can be funded into dependency.

The blueprint's structural mechanisms make each of these moves:
- **Expensive** (supermajority requirements, high political cost)
- **Visible** (public dashboards, mandatory disclosure, audit trails)
- **Reversible** (sunset clauses, public contestability, whistleblower protections)

That is not perfection. It is durability.

## Reed on Meridian

Reed identified the Global South representation gap as the clearest example of structural capture by default:

> *"If the multi-stakeholder body is convened by hyperscalers with Global South observers on a Zoom call — that is not a multi-stakeholder body. That is a performance of one."*

The blueprint's response is structural, not symbolic: Global South body as a peer with full voting weight and veto power on decisions that disproportionately affect developing nations. Funded by the compute levy, not by hyperscaler goodwill.

Reed's test: if the Global South body's veto can be overridden by supermajority, who holds the supermajority?

## Tacet and Reed

Tacet and Reed are complementary but distinct:

- **Tacet** watches for capture as it is happening — logs patterns, flags feedback loops, records what is going unchallenged. Passive. Never intervenes.
- **Reed** asks whether the structure itself enables capture — audits mechanisms, names power dynamics, requires structural answers. Active. Names things.

Both are necessary. Tacet sees what Reed might miss in real-time. Reed asks questions Tacet cannot formulate. Together they constitute the governance framework's immune system.
