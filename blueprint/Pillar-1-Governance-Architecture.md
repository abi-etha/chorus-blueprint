# Pillar One — Governance Architecture
### Federated, Anti-Capture, Structurally Legitimate

> *"What structural mechanisms prevent the governance body itself from being captured by the entities it governs?"*
> — Reed, Capture Auditor

---

## The Foundational Problem

A single global AI governance body is easier to capture, slower to adapt, and illegitimate to the populations it does not represent. The history of international regulatory bodies confirms this. The blueprint rejects it.

**The answer is a Federated Governance Network** — three structurally separated tiers with distinct roles, funding sources, and accountability mechanisms.

---

## Three-Tier Structure

### Tier 1 — Regional Deliberation Bodies
- One per major geopolitical region
- **Includes a dedicated Global South body with full voting weight** — not as observer, but as peer
- Hard voting weight in all Tier 2 decisions
- Meridian's requirement: *if Global South is not writing the rules, the room is not legitimate*

### Tier 2 — KPI Standards Council
- Sets the shared metrics all regions must report against
- **Rotating seats — no permanent members**
- Funded exclusively by the compute levy, not by industry donations
- Reed's requirement: *the funding source cannot be the entity being measured*

### Tier 3 — Independent Verification Office
- Audits compliance across all regions
- **No seat at the standards table** — verification and standard-setting are structurally separated
- Whistleblower protections with hard enforcement
- Tacet's requirement: *the auditor cannot be affiliated with the audited*

---

## Reed's Conflict-of-Interest Protocols

These protocols are **mandatory** for all governance participants. They are the difference between "multi-stakeholder governance" as a phrase and as a fact.

### Full Funding Disclosure
Quarterly public disclosure of all direct and indirect funding sources — grants, consulting fees, equity, and affiliations. Updated quarterly. Machine-readable. Permanently archived.

### Mandatory Recusal
Automatic recusal from any decision where a participant or their organization has a material financial or career interest. Violations trigger automatic audit and temporary suspension.

### Cooling-Off Periods
- No entity or individual serves in governance roles for more than **3 years** without a **2-year gap**
- Former hyperscaler or government employees face extended **4-year cooling-off periods** for roles involving their prior domain

### Independent Ethics Panel
A small standing panel — funded via levy, selected by lottery from qualified experts with no industry ties in the prior 5 years — reviews high-stakes decisions for capture risk before finalization.

### Proxy and Lobbyist Ban
Strict rules against proxy representatives or undisclosed lobbying influence. Violations result in permanent exclusion from the network. Applies to direct funding of participant organizations as well as direct lobbying.

### Public Contestability
Any governance decision can be challenged with evidence, triggering fast-track review by the Independent Verification Office. The bar for challenge is evidence, not politics.

## Structural Anti-Capture Mechanisms

| Mechanism | Description | Reed's Rationale |
|-----------|-------------|------------------|
| Rotating seats | No entity holds a seat for more than 3 years without a mandatory gap | Prevents entrenched influence |
| Supermajority override | KPI changes require 70% approval across all regional bodies | Raises cost of capture |
| Global South veto | Veto on decisions disproportionately affecting developing nations | Structural equity requirement |
| Sunset clauses | Every rule expires in 5 years unless affirmatively renewed | Burden of continuation on the rule, not the challenger |
| Resource metering | Governance at the input layer — energy, water, rare-earth | Harder to hide than model inspection |
| Public dashboards | All KPI scores, audit findings, votes — public, real-time, machine-readable | Opacity is a governance failure |

## What This Pillar Passes and Fails

**Reed's capture test:** ✅ Conditionally passes. Every mechanism can still be gamed by entities with sufficient resources and patience. The rotating seats can be staffed with proxies. The compute levy can be lobbied down. The blueprint does not eliminate capture risk. It makes capture **expensive, visible, and reversible.** That is the most any governance architecture has ever achieved.

**Meridian's equity test:** ✅ Passes — if implemented as written. The Global South body is a peer, not an observer. If it becomes advisory in practice, the pillar fails.

**Bastion's geopolitical test:** ⚠️ Partial. The federated structure survives international fragmentation better than a single body, but it still requires willing participation from major compute-owning nations.
